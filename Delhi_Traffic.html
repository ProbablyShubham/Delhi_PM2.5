The idea behind this project is to plot a map of the PM 2.5 data collected in Delhi across its air quality monitoring stations between 2006-2018 and perform statistical analysis on the same to derive insights using R. The key questions that this analysis intends to answer are:

    1. Does traffic time matter specifically at choke points?
    1. Are choke point stations generally more polluted than others?
    1. Is the difference between Peak and Off-Peak significantly larger at choke points compared to control stations?

To do this, air quality monitoring stations across Delhi are plotted alongside a shortlisted set of traffic choke points in Delhi. The PM 2.5 monitoring stations near (within a 2 km radius) these choke points are identified and compared against the rest of the dataset using a linear model regression.

bb_delhi <- getbb("Delhi, India") 
delhi_boundary_raw <- opq(bb_delhi) %>%
  add_osm_feature(key = "admin_level", value = "4") %>%
  add_osm_feature(key = "name", value = "Delhi") %>%
  osmdata_sf()

delhi_boundary <- delhi_boundary_raw$osm_multipolygons

delhi_boundary <- st_union(delhi_boundary) %>% st_as_sf() # Dissolve points into a single polygon

target_crs <- 32643  # UTM zone 43N (Delhi) to ensure accuracy in measuring distances | https://epsg.io/32643

stations_utm       <- st_transform(stations_sf, target_crs)
choke_utm          <- st_transform(choke_sf, target_crs)
delhi_boundary_utm <- st_transform(delhi_boundary, target_crs)

dist_threshold <- set_units(2, "km") #Distance threshold was chosen as 2 km as a reasonable estimate

Final Discussion:

The analysis shows a significant correlation between spatial location and PM 2.5 levels, as stations situated within 2km of identified traffic choke points registered significantly higher baseline pollution levels (+53.7 units) compared to control stations. Contrary to the hypothesis that rush-hour traffic causes spikes in pollution, the temporal analysis showed that â€œEvening Peaksâ€ actually experienced lower PM 2.5 levels than the â€œOff-Peakâ€ baseline.

Note: The Adj. RÂ² value is consistently equal to 0.00 across all models, thereby suggesting that traffic dynamics alone are poor predictors of air quality in Delhi - especially when considering a scale of nearly 1 million measurements across 12 years. Among other factors, stubble burning in neighboring states is a known driver of PM 2.5 levels across Delhi. More scientifically rigorous models are required to correctly identify the drivers of PM 2.5 pollution in Delhi.
